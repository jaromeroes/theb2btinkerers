Why 65% of B2B marketing budgets deliver no measurable business impact

The misalignment problem isn't new. But it's getting more expensive — and most companies are still solving it wrong.

T
The B2B Tinkerers
January 2025 8 min read

  • Not a people problem
  • Failure 1: Objectives set in isolation
  • Failure 2: Wrong metrics reported up
  • What actually fixes it
Recognise your company in this? Let's talk.

In 2024, Forrester asked over 1,200 B2B marketing and sales leaders a simple question: are your marketing and business objectives aligned? 65% said no. And yet, in the same survey, 82% of C-suite executives said they believed alignment was strong at their company.

That gap — 17 percentage points between what leaders believe and what the teams on the ground experience — is the core of the problem. It's not a data problem. It's a communication problem. And it costs more than most companies realise.

The misalignment isn't about people

The first thing most companies get wrong is assuming this is a talent problem. If only we had better marketers, or a stronger sales team, or a more engaged CMO — then it would work.

It's not a talent problem. In twelve years of running marketing teams across B2B tech and industrial companies, I've never encountered a case where misalignment came down to someone being bad at their job. It always came down to one of three structural failures.

"Marketing teams measure activity. CEOs need to see impact on the business. Nobody has built the bridge between the two — and that gap is costing B2B companies real money."

— The B2B Tinkerers

Failure 1: Marketing objectives are set without the business

The planning process goes like this: the CEO sets business targets, the CFO allocates budgets by department, and marketing takes its budget allocation to build a plan. The marketing plan is then reviewed in isolation — by marketing leadership, sometimes by the CMO, occasionally by the CEO for a 20-minute slide deck review.

Nobody asks the fundamental question: does every line item in this plan connect to a specific business objective? The answer, almost universally, is no. Not because the marketers don't care — but because the process doesn't require it.

Failure 2: The wrong metrics are reported upward

Marketing reports MQLs, impressions, open rates, and website traffic. The CEO cares about pipeline, revenue, and market share. When marketing reports a record quarter — highest traffic ever, best email performance in years — and the CEO looks at the pipeline and sees nothing moved, the relationship breaks down.

The fix isn't to stop measuring what marketing can control. It's to translate those metrics into the language the business speaks — and to be honest when the translation doesn't hold up.

What actually fixes it

The companies that get alignment right do three things differently:

  • They build marketing plans top-down from business objectives, not bottom-up from marketing budgets. Every tactic traces back to a business goal.
  • They report on business impact, not marketing activity. This requires some difficult conversations early on — when you can't yet show that impact — but it builds the credibility that makes the relationship work long-term.
  • They have someone in the room who speaks both languages. Someone who understands what the CEO needs from the business, and what the marketing team is capable of delivering. That's the role. Whether it's an internal hire, a VP, or a fractional CMO — it's the same function.

The companies we work with aren't failing because their marketing team is weak. They're failing because nobody has connected the two sides of the conversation. That's the work. And it's simpler than most people expect — once you know where to start.
